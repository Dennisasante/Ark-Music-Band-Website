TERMS OF USE

Lignin is the most abundant and complex bio-polymer in nature because of its low biodegradability due to chemical bonding between its monomers resulting in recalcitrant compound of ligninocellulosic materials. Lignin consists of high molecular weight aromatic polymer, containing numerous biologically stable ether or ester linkages. The degradation of lignin is important for industrial purposes. Lignin degrading enzymes have been divided into 2 main groups. These are lignin degrading auxiliary enzymes and lignin modifying enzymes. Lignin degrading auxiliary enzymes are unable to degrage lignin on their own and so require additional enzyme involvement for complete degradation. Lignin degrading auxiliary enzymes enable the process of lignin degradation through the sequential action of several proteins that may include oxidative H2O2. Lignin degrading auxiliary enzymes include cellubiose dehydrogenase, aryl alcohol oxidase, glyoxal oxidase and pyranose-2-oxidase while laccase, haeme-containing peroxidases like lignin peroxidase, manganese peroxidase and versatile peroxidase and feruloyl esterase are lignin modifying enzymes and ligninolytic enzymes. Laccase catalyzes the overall reaction 4 Benzenediol + O2 to give 4 Benzosemiquinone + 2H2O. Lignin peroxidase is a glycoprotein containing one mole of iron protoporphorin IX per molecule of protein which catalyzes the H2O2 dependant oxidative polymerization of lignin. Manganese peroxidase is a glycosylated haem protein that depolymerizes lignin molecule in the presence of manganese ion. The versatile peroxidase has broad range substrate sharing typical features of the manganese and lignin peroxidase families. The activities of these enzymes are enhanced by feruroyl esterase, aryl-alcohol dehydrogenase, quinone reductases and lipases that facilitate the process for degradation and detoxification of lignocellulosic waste in environment.
